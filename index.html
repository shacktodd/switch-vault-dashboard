Abstract
Research question: Under what conditions does financial coercion produce behavioral capitulation versus strategic indigenization — and what are the systemic consequences when it consistently produces the latter?

This research program investigates a structural bifurcation in the international monetary system: the simultaneous expansion of programmable, surveillance-capable "control layer" instruments (central bank digital currencies, regulated stablecoins, multi-CBDC settlement platforms) and opaque, seizure-resistant "exit layer" assets (repatriated gold reserves, self-custody crypto, off-book state finance). The framework introduces two original analytical constructs — Financial Strategic Depth (FSD), a resilience-over-constraint ratio for state-level monetary independence, and Criminal Strategic Depth (CSD), its analog for illicit and sanctioned networks — and applies them to the post-2022 global monetary environment triggered by the ~$300B Russian sovereign asset freeze. Six operational indicators (I1–I6) track real-world threshold crossings against named primary data series, updated on a weekly cadence. Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH) methodology assigns probability ranges to four structural outcome scenarios. The framework identifies a reflexive dynamic — the Imposition Paradox — in which coercion-failure systematically strengthens the capacity it targets. The site presents the live indicator board, underlying analytical framework, and theoretical grounding as of the current review period.

This dashboard tracks a research framework called The Switch and the Vault. The big idea: the world's financial system is quietly splitting in two — a control layer (money governments can watch and freeze) and an exit layer (money that's harder to track or seize). Both sides are growing at the same time, and for the same reason.

The six indicators below (I1–I6) are early-warning signals for how far this split has gone. Here's what each part of the framework means in plain language.

The Switch

The "Switch" is a new kind of money that governments can program — like a debit card the government can freeze, limit, or track, but built into the currency itself.

Examples: China's digital yuan (e-CNY), which the government can restrict to certain stores or expire if not spent. The U.S. GENIUS Act stablecoins, which give Treasury the power to freeze any wallet.

Analogy: Imagine your cash could only be spent at approved stores and expired in 30 days. That's what programmable money makes possible.

The Vault

The "Vault" is the opposite reaction. As governments build more control into money, countries are moving wealth into things harder to seize — gold stored at home, Bitcoin, and off-the-books financial systems.

Example: France moved 129 tons of gold from the U.S. Federal Reserve back to Paris. India moved 274 tons home. They don't want someone else holding their savings.

Analogy: If your bank could freeze your account anytime, you'd keep some cash at home. Countries are doing the same thing — at national scale.

Why both are happening at once

In 2022, the U.S. and Europe froze roughly $300 billion in Russian government assets — the largest financial seizure in history. Every other country watched and thought: "That could happen to us."

So they started building escape routes (the Vault) while the U.S. doubled down on financial control tools (the Switch).

Core paradox: the more the control layer tightens, the more valuable the exit layer becomes — which justifies even tighter controls. It's a feedback loop.

Financial Strategic Depth (FSD)

FSD is a score for how financially independent a country is — how long it could function if the U.S. and allies tried to cut it off economically. It measures what a country has built (payment systems, gold reserves, trade networks) versus the pressure it faces (sanctions, market cutoffs).

Analogy: "If all your neighbors stopped doing business with you, how long could you survive?" A high FSD score means a long time.

Criminal Strategic Depth (CSD)

CSD applies the same idea to illegal or sanctioned networks. Key finding: North Korea stole $577M in crypto in early 2026. They laundered it through the same Chinese banking networks used by Mexican drug cartels and Russian sanctions evaders — simultaneously. Attribution complexity is multiplicative, not additive.

Analogy: The same laundromats washing money for the mob, a drug cartel, and a foreign spy agency at once. Shutting one down is complicated because you'd expose you were watching all three.

Corporate FSD Exposure

This measures how much a company is at risk from U.S.-China tensions — and the surprising finding is that China revenue share tells you almost nothing about actual risk. SpaceX makes nearly zero money in China but has Very High exposure through satellite competition, rare-earth dependence, and principal capture via Musk/Tesla.

Analogy: You don't have to do business with someone for them to have power over you. If they control something you depend on, they have leverage regardless.

The Imposition Paradox

When you put economic pressure on a country, you expect it to back down. The paradox is that it often does the opposite. U.S. semiconductor sanctions pushed China to build its own chip industry. The Russian reserve freeze pushed countries worldwide to build dollar-bypass payment systems. Pressure created the very capability it was trying to prevent.

Analogy: Squeezing a water balloon doesn't deflate it — it moves the water somewhere else.

The Great Repricing

For 30 years, the world rewarded efficiency — cut costs, specialize, depend on global supply chains. Now the world is rewarding resilience. Gold, domestic factories, backup supply chains, rare-earth stockpiles: things that look wasteful in peacetime are now strategic assets. The market is repricing everything accordingly.

Analogy: Nobody kept a spare tire because breakdowns were rare. Then the roads got bad and everyone wanted a spare again. Same logic, applied to entire economies.

The Price of Slack

"Slack" means spare capacity — backup suppliers, extra inventory, redundant systems. For decades, businesses eliminated slack because it cost money. COVID showed what happens without it: one factory shutting down stopped the entire global car industry because nobody kept spare chips.

Analogy: Running an engine at maximum RPM is efficient — until it blows. Keeping a little slack extends its life. Economies work the same way, on longer cycles.

The ACH table (H1–H4)

ACH (Analysis of Competing Hypotheses) is a structured method that assigns probability ranges to four different future scenarios rather than picking one answer. The base case (H2, 55–70%) is "Bounded Multipolarity" — no single winner, the dollar loses its monopoly, and the world splits into competing financial blocs.

Like a weather forecast: "70% chance of rain, 30% chance of a thunderstorm." Both are possible. You plan for both.

The CSD/FSD framework builds on, extends, and in several cases departs from the following bodies of scholarship. Where the framework departs from prior work, the departure is the analytical contribution.

Hirschman — Exit, Voice, and Loyalty

Hirschman, A.O. (1970). Exit, Voice, and Loyalty. Harvard University Press.

Provides the foundational behavioral mechanism for the Imposition Paradox. Hirschman predicts that actors facing coercion exercise exit (find alternatives) or voice (negotiate), not simply capitulate. The CSD/FSD framework operationalizes this as a measurable ratio: when exit capacity (FSD numerator) grows faster than pressure (denominator), coercion fails.

Framework relationship: BUILDS ON — formalizes Hirschman's qualitative prediction as a quantitative resilience ratio.

Farrell & Newman — Weaponized Interdependence

Farrell, H. & Newman, A. (2019). Weaponized Interdependence. International Security, 44(1).

Establishes that states weaponize hub positions in global networks (financial, informational) to coerce periphery actors. The CSD/FSD framework extends this to the corporate level (Corporate FSD Exposure) and adds the critical finding that exposure is orthogonal to revenue geography — a company can be deeply exposed without any direct network position in the target country.

Framework relationship: EXTENDS — adds corporate-level measurement and the orthogonality finding not present in Farrell/Newman.

Kindleberger — Hegemonic Stability Theory

Kindleberger, C. (1973). The World in Depression 1929–1939. University of California Press.

Predicts that the international monetary system requires a single hegemon to provide liquidity and enforce rules. The H2 base case (Bounded Multipolarity) is a direct challenge to Kindleberger's prediction — the framework argues the system can persist in a multipolar configuration, though with higher friction and tail risk.

Framework relationship: DEPARTS — H2 base case explicitly argues against Kindleberger's single-hegemon necessity claim.

Pozsar — Multipolar Money

Pozsar, Z. (2022–2023). Global Money Notes. Credit Suisse / Ex Uno Plures.

Pozsar identifies Bretton Woods III — a commodity-backed multipolar reserve system emerging from the 2022 shock — as the successor to the dollar-credit system. The Switch and Vault framework is broadly consistent with Pozsar's monetary architecture diagnosis, while adding the reflexive control-vs-exit dynamic and the CSD/FSD operational measurement layer that Pozsar's framework lacks.

Framework relationship: CONSISTENT WITH — adds operational indicators and the reflexive feedback mechanism.

Arrighi — Long Twentieth Century

Arrighi, G. (1994). The Long Twentieth Century. Verso.

Documents successive hegemon cycles (Genoa → Netherlands → Britain → United States), each characterized by a signal crisis (financialization, overextension) followed by terminal crisis and handoff. The framework's Historical Lineage thesis draws directly on Arrighi's cycle structure, extending it to the current transition with the 2022 reserve freeze as the potential signal crisis event.

Framework relationship: BUILDS ON — applies Arrighi's cycle structure to current events with specific falsification criteria.

Minsky — Financial Fragility

Minsky, H. (1982). Can "It" Happen Again? M.E. Sharpe.

Minsky's financial instability hypothesis — that stability breeds instability as actors take on increasing risk during calm periods — directly underpins the Price of Slack framework. The 1990–2020 globalization era is characterized as a Minsky-style "short-volatility carry trade": selling redundancy (slack) and collecting efficiency gains until realized volatility exceeded the premium.

Framework relationship: BUILDS ON — Price of Slack formalizes Minsky's instability mechanism in a resilience/efficiency context.

Polanyi — The Great Transformation

Polanyi, K. (1944). The Great Transformation. Farrar & Rinehart.

Polanyi's "double movement" — market expansion generating a self-protective counter-movement — maps directly onto the Switch/Vault dynamic. The exit-layer flight (gold repatriation, CBDC alternatives, crypto self-custody) is precisely the Polanyian self-protection response to the market's overreach into monetary sovereignty. The framework operationalizes when the counter-movement reaches critical mass.

Framework relationship: BUILDS ON — operationalizes Polanyi's double movement with named data thresholds.

Attali — Succession of Dominant Cores

Attali, J. (2009). A Brief History of the Future. Arcade Publishing.

Attali documents successive dominant mercantile/financial cores (Bruges → Venice → Antwerp → Genoa → Amsterdam → London → New York), each bankrupted by military overextension and replaced by the next core. Provides mainstream-credentialed independent corroboration of the framework's cyclicality claim — the same mechanism arrived at independently across completely different scholarly traditions.

Framework relationship: CORROBORATES — independent convergence on cyclicality from a mainstream policy-adjacent source.

I1–I6 Indicator Construction

Each indicator is constructed with four required components: (1) a named primary data series, (2) a mechanical trigger threshold, (3) a disconfirm condition specifying what would falsify the trigger, and (4) an update cadence and method (auto-fetch or manual review). Thresholds were set to be minimally sufficient — the weakest evidence consistent with the claim — not maximally demanding. Indicators are binary (TRIGGERED/PARTIAL/WATCH), not continuous scores, to prevent false precision.

Epistemic Labeling Protocol

All load-bearing claims carry one of three labels:

  • DOCUMENTED Primary or corroborated secondary source; claim would survive peer review scrutiny.
  • CONTESTED Genuine scholarly disagreement exists; the claim reflects a reasonable interpretation, not consensus.
  • SPECULATIVE Framework extrapolation beyond available data; directionally plausible but not yet evidenced.

Conspiratorial or banking-cabal framing is quarantined as belief-psychology evidence only — never used as a causal mechanism.

ACH Probability Scoring

Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH) follows the IC analytic standards methodology. Probability ranges (not point estimates) reflect genuine uncertainty. Scoring criteria: consistency of each hypothesis with all triggered indicators, plausibility of the causal chain, and the number of load-bearing assumptions required. H2 (Bounded Multipolarity) has the highest probability range because it requires the fewest auxiliary assumptions — it is the residual after ruling out cleaner outcomes. H4 is a tail overlay, not mutually exclusive with H1–H3.

Falsification Standards

The framework is designed to be falsifiable. Each indicator has a named disconfirm condition. The framework as a whole would be falsified if: (a) USD reserve share rebounds above 63% in two consecutive COFER quarters; (b) central-bank gold purchases fall below 400t/year; (c) Iran, Russia, or DPRK demonstrably alters strategic behavior in direct response to economic pressure within a 6-month window. None of these conditions have been met as of the current review period.

Update Cadence

Automatable indicators (I2 — IMF COFER, FRED gold/dollar series) update weekly via GitHub Actions. Manual indicators (I1, I3, I4, I5, I6) require human review before update — edit manual_overrides.json, commit, and trigger workflow. Version history is maintained in data.json and displayed in the Version History section below. The board is not a predictive model; it is a structured observation system. All readings reflect the state at the date displayed.

CSD/FSD Formula Notes

FSD = (αP + βC + γG + δT + Ipar) / ((S + E + M) · Φ) where numerator terms are payments sovereignty (P), credit substitution (C), governance substitution (G), technological indigenization (T), and parallel channels (Ipar); denominator terms are sanctions (S), enforcement (E), market dependency (M), multiplied by bidirectional friction Φ ∈ (0,1]. FSD > 1 indicates the system preserves function faster than pressure degrades it. All α/β/γ/δ weights are analytical constructs, not empirically calibrated measurements — false precision is explicitly disclaimed.
